Endogenous Photosynthesis in a Vertebrate Model via Horizontal Plastid Integration and Behavioral Sunbathing Optimization

Abstract

In this study, we report the first successful integration of functional chloroplasts into the somatic cells of a vertebrate model organism, enabling endogenous photosynthesis and partial autotrophy under standard sunlight exposure. Using a proprietary blend of kale smoothies and CRISPR-based gene shuffling, we achieved stable expression of rbcL, psaA, and a sunscreen-suppressed version of melanin. The modified animals exhibited reduced food intake, elevated chlorophyll fluorescence, and a noticeable preference for south-facing rocks.

Survival was sustained up to 3 days under continuous illumination, after which the subject attempted to photosynthesize its own Netflix subscription. These findings suggest a promising future in which sunlight replaces lunch, and tanning becomes a dietary requirement. Further studies are required to determine the optimal SPF for peak ATP yield.
